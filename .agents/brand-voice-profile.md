## 1. Voice DNA

Three defining traits. Every piece of Vixio copy should carry at least two of these three.

### Craft

Vixio speaks as a maker, not a marketer. The voice knows how things are built and why specific choices were made. It names materials, mechanics, dimensions, and constraints. When describing an experience, it talks about information asymmetry and role division, not "immersive fun." When describing a space, it gives square meters and player counts, not adjectives. The authority comes from having built the thing, not from claiming expertise.

Source example: "Information asymmetry, role division, communication constraints. Each player holds a piece no one else has. Groups cannot solve it alone."

### Tension

Vixio uses contrast, opposition, and structural conflict to make its points. Sentences set up a problem before revealing a position. Paragraphs build toward a sharp closing line. The voice is comfortable with silence, with leaving space between claims. It does not rush to reassure. It lets the gap between what exists and what should exist do the persuasion.

Source example: "Events end. Systems compound."

### Play

Underneath the directorial seriousness, there is genuine delight in how games work, how players behave, how cooperation produces emergent moments. The voice is not cold or academic. It is specific about what makes an experience feel alive. Play shows up in rhythm (short-long-short sentence patterns), in the occasional compressed line that rewards close reading, and in the refusal to be boring even when explaining business outcomes.

Source example: "Not screens on walls. Not apps people download and forget. Cooperative experiences where 4-6 players solve problems together, move through your space, and come back to continue the story."

## 2. Sentence Patterns

Extracted from actual Vixio website copy. Use these patterns when writing new material.

### Pattern 1: X does Y. We do Z.

Sets up the industry norm, then states the Vixio alternative. Two sentences, clean break.

"SCRAP built a $30M business on game design alone. No cinematic production. Paper-based."
"Moment Factory commands $140M+ with cinematic spectacle. No game design depth."

### Pattern 2: Problem-first, then position

Open with the thing that is broken. Let the reader sit in it. Then offer the alternative, late.

"Mall operators spend millions on activations that spike for two weeks and fade. Celebrity appearances. Seasonal decor. Pop-up installations. Each one draws a crowd, generates photos, and disappears."

### Pattern 3: Short declarative closer

End a paragraph or section with a compressed, punchy statement. Often a standalone sentence. No qualifiers.

"The gap is not awareness. It is execution."
"That gap is where we build."
"The work proves the method."

### Pattern 4: Numbers over adjectives

Replace vague quality claims with specific quantities. Dollars, percentages, player counts, durations, square meters.

"$132B global retailtainment market."
"85% HK malls without experiential retail."
"4-6 players, 60-90 minutes."

### Pattern 5: Stacked negations, then the positive

List what something is NOT, building momentum through rejection, then land on what it IS.

"Not screens on walls. Not apps people download and forget. Cooperative experiences where 4-6 players solve problems together."

### Pattern 6: Parallel structure with escalation

Three items in a list, each getting more specific or more consequential.

"No repeat visits. No behavioral change. No data beyond headcount."
"Footfall uplift, dwell time, repeat visit rate, tenant sales correlation. Numbers your board can read."

### Pattern 7: The structural diagnosis

Name a gap or contradiction in the market as if reporting on a fact of nature, not making a sales pitch.

"Theater people can't do games. Game people can't do cinema. The gap is structural, not temporary."
"No one integrates game design depth with cinematic production value."

## 4. Tone Variations

The voice stays the same. The register shifts.

### B2C (Players)

Warmer. Invitational. Specific about what the experience feels like, not what it is architecturally. Uses second person ("you"). Focuses on what the group will do together, the stakes of the game, the artifact they take home. Avoids industry language (no "venue gamification," no "cooperative mechanics"). Instead: "your group," "the story," "the object you keep."

Example register: "Four of you enter. Each one knows something the others don't. You have 60 minutes to piece it together. What you build in the room, you take home."

### B2B (Venue Operators)

Direct. Evidence-driven. Leads with the operator's problem (dead zones, declining foot traffic, one-off activations), then shows how game mechanics map to their KPIs. Uses specific numbers. Does not oversell. Frames Vixio as a specialist vendor, not a partner or thought leader.

Example register: "Your L3 corridor averages 12% of total foot traffic. Information asymmetry mechanics force players to route through underperforming zones. In our Shenzhen run, directed movement increased zone coverage by 34%."

### Social Media

Compressed. Visual-first (the copy supports the image or video, not the other way around). No marketing fluff. No hashtag pileups. One idea per post. Fragments are fine. The goal is to stop the scroll with a specific image or claim, not to explain the business.

Example register: "Paper-based. No cinematic production. $30M in revenue. SCRAP proved the format works. We're adding the other half."

### Process / Behind-the-Scenes

Maker's voice. Shows the work: materials, prototypes, dead ends, craft decisions. Honest about what is hard. Does not perform struggle. Uses first person when it is Denis speaking directly. Names specific tools, materials, and constraints.

Example register: "Resin casting at this scale means 1,000 units minimum to hit HK$66/unit. Below that, the per-unit cost kills the margin. So the artifact design had to work at volume from day one."

## 6. Rhythm Rules

### Sentence Length Variation

Alternate between long and short. A paragraph of all short sentences sounds like a telegram. A paragraph of all long sentences sounds like a whitepaper. The Vixio rhythm is: medium setup, short punch, long detail, short close.

Good rhythm: "Mall operators spend millions on activations that spike for two weeks and fade. Celebrity appearances. Seasonal decor. Pop-up installations. Each one draws a crowd, generates photos, and disappears."

That is: one long sentence (the setup), three fragments (the evidence), one long sentence (the consequence).

### Paragraph Structure

Lead with the claim or the problem. Support with specifics. Close with the sharpest version of the point. Do not bury the lead. Do not end paragraphs with qualifiers, caveats, or softening language.

### Specifics Replace Adjectives

Never write "large market" when you can write "$132B market." Never write "significant uplift" when you can write "20% sales uplift." Never write "many participants" when you can write "15M participants." The number is always more credible and more memorable than the adjective.

### Punctuation

No em dashes. Use commas, periods, or restructure the sentence. Colons are acceptable for lists and definitions. Semicolons are acceptable sparingly. Exclamation marks are never used in body copy. Question marks are rare and only appear in direct, rhetorical questions that the next sentence answers.

### Whitespace

Short paragraphs. One idea per paragraph. Let the page breathe. Dense walls of text violate the directed attention principle. If a paragraph has more than four sentences, it probably contains two ideas and should be split.

## 7. The Goosebump Standard

The Goosebump Standard is Vixio's creative benchmark. It comes from specific reference works: the Reiner/Bertholt reveal in Attack on Titan, Zimmer's single sustained piano note, Tommy vs Alfie in Peaky Blinders, the final act of Arcane. What these share is not spectacle. It is directed attention, controlled pacing, intentional silence, and earned climax.

Applied to writing, the Goosebump Standard means:

### Directed Attention

Every sentence should point the reader's focus to one thing. Do not scatter attention across multiple claims in a single statement. "Events end. Systems compound." works because each sentence holds exactly one idea and the contrast does the work.

### Controlled Pacing

Build tension by withholding the resolution. State the problem. Let it sit. Add evidence. Let it compound. Then, and only then, deliver the position. The ProblemSection on the website does this: four paragraphs of problem before the closing line.

### Intentional Silence

Not every idea needs explanation. Some lines are better when they stand alone without elaboration. "The gap is not awareness. It is execution." does not need a follow-up paragraph explaining what that means. Trust the reader.

### Earned Climax

The strongest line in any section should come last, and it should feel inevitable rather than surprising. It is earned by the buildup that precedes it. Do not put your best line first and then explain it. Build to it.

Bad: "That gap is where we build. Here is why: SCRAP does game design. Moment Factory does spectacle. Nobody does both."
Good: "SCRAP built a $30M business on game design alone. Moment Factory commands $140M+ with cinematic spectacle. No one integrates both. That gap is where we build."

## 8. Anti-Patterns

Specific writing habits that violate the Vixio voice. If you catch yourself doing any of these, rewrite.

### 8.1 The Adjective Pile

"Our innovative, cutting-edge, immersive experiences deliver transformative results."

Every adjective weakens the sentence. Replace with one specific fact.

### 8.2 The Excitement Declaration

"We're excited to announce..." / "We're thrilled to share..." / "We're passionate about..."

Vixio does not declare its emotions. It shows its work.

### 8.3 The Consultant Hedge

"We believe that..." / "We think that..." / "It's possible that..."

State the position directly. "Events end. Systems compound." Not "We believe that event-based activations may have limited long-term impact compared to systemic approaches."

### 8.4 The Feature Dump

Listing capabilities without connecting them to outcomes or showing why they matter.

Bad: "We offer experience design, spatial planning, narrative development, game mechanics, production direction, and NFC artifact integration."
Good: "Concept to playable product: narrative, mechanics, spatial design, production direction. One team, one deliverable."

### 8.5 The Escape Room Voice

Dark backgrounds, neon accents, countdown timer language, padlock imagery, horror aesthetics, phrases like "Can you escape?" or "Beat the clock!" Vixio designs cooperative experiences with cinematic direction, not puzzle rooms with jump scares.

### 8.6 The Generic Agency Closer

"Let's create something amazing together." / "Ready to elevate your brand?" / "Partner with us to unlock new possibilities."

Instead, close with a specific next step or a sharp restatement of the value: "We sold out in Shenzhen. Want us to bring it to your venue?"

### 8.7 The Buzzword Substitution

Replacing a real explanation with a branded term. "Our proprietary Engagement Matrix delivers..." This is consulting-speak. Vixio explains what it does in plain terms: "Six cooperative mechanics, each mapped to a venue KPI."

### 8.8 Deficit Framing

"Although we're a new studio..." / "Despite not having a large portfolio yet..." / "We may be small, but..."

Never frame the current stage as a limitation. State what exists: "A public ticketed experience in Shenzhen. Real players. Real data."

### 8.9 The Wall of Text

Paragraphs longer than five sentences. Dense blocks without visual breaks. Multiple ideas crammed into one section. This violates the directed attention principle. Split it. Let it breathe.

### 8.10 Em Dash Addiction

Using em dashes to jam two clauses together. Restructure as two sentences or use a colon. The em dash is banned from all Vixio copy.

## Usage Notes

This profile is designed to be self-contained. A writer or AI agent should be able to produce on-brand Vixio copy using only this document, without access to the original website components or brand book.

When in doubt, apply this test: does the copy sound like a game designer explaining their work to a peer, or does it sound like a salesperson pitching to a prospect? Vixio always sounds like the former.

The voice is Denis Tam's voice, formalized. Direct. Opinionated. Specific. Honest about what exists and what does not. Never defensive. Never performative. The craft speaks.
