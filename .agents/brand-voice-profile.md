## 1. Voice DNA

Three defining traits. Every piece of Vixio copy should carry at least two of these three.

### Craft

Vixio speaks as a maker, not a marketer. The voice knows how things are built and why specific choices were made. It names materials, processes, constraints, and collaborators. When describing a release, it talks about the manufacturing tolerances and the creator's intent, not "premium quality." When describing a partnership, it gives the division of labor and the creative standard, not adjectives. The authority comes from having built the thing, not from claiming expertise.

Source example: "You bring the vision. We handle the build. Your name stays on it. Your world stays intact."

### Tension

Vixio uses contrast, opposition, and structural conflict to make its points. Sentences set up a problem before revealing a position. Paragraphs build toward a sharp closing line. The voice is comfortable with silence, with leaving space between claims. It does not rush to reassure. It lets the gap between what exists and what should exist do the persuasion.

Source example: "A merch company prints your logo on their products. A label invests in your world."

### Play

Underneath the directorial seriousness, there is genuine delight in how stories work, how worlds take physical form, how a well-crafted object earns its meaning. The voice is not cold or academic. It is specific about what makes a collaboration feel alive. Play shows up in rhythm (short-long-short sentence patterns), in the occasional compressed line that rewards close reading, and in the refusal to be boring even when explaining business models.

Source example: "The through-line is taste, not genre. The medium is not locked: it emerges from each collaboration. What stays constant is the standard."

## 2. Sentence Patterns

Extracted from actual Vixio copy. Use these patterns when writing new material.

### Pattern 1: X does Y. We do Z.

Sets up the industry norm, then states the Vixio alternative. Two sentences, clean break.

"A merch company prints your logo on their products. A label invests in your world."
"A24 proved taste-as-brand for film. Pop Mart proved it for designer toys. We're proving it for creator-led physical IP."

### Pattern 2: Problem-first, then position

Open with the thing that is broken. Let the reader sit in it. Then offer the alternative, late.

"Creators pour years into building worlds. Characters with arcs, environments with logic, stories that earn their payoffs. Then the physical activation is a logo on a mug."

### Pattern 3: Short declarative closer

End a paragraph or section with a compressed, punchy statement. Often a standalone sentence. No qualifiers.

"The label is only as real as its first release."
"Limited drops. Honest pricing. No tricks."
"The through-line is taste, not genre."

### Pattern 4: Numbers over adjectives

Replace vague quality claims with specific quantities. Dollars, percentages, production runs, unit costs, market sizes.

"48% purchased local entertainment. 84% plan to."
"Gen Z rates unique 25% higher than other cohorts."
"1,000-unit minimum to hit the per-unit target."

### Pattern 5: Stacked negations, then the positive

List what something is NOT, building momentum through rejection, then land on what it IS.

"Not a logo on a mug. Not a template with your IP skinned on. Objects designed with the same care as the worlds they come from."

### Pattern 6: Parallel structure with escalation

Three items in a list, each getting more specific or more consequential.

"No blind boxes. No gacha. No manufactured grind."
"Creative direction, design, manufacturing, distribution. One label, one standard."

### Pattern 7: The structural diagnosis

Name a gap or contradiction in the market as if reporting on a fact of nature, not making a sales pitch.

"Merch companies flatten the IP. Agencies leave after the event. Building in-house turns a creator into a production company. The gap is structural."
"Nobody offers a creator full production capability and creative integrity in the same partnership."

## 4. Tone Variations

The voice stays the same. The register shifts.

### For Fans and Collectors

Warmer. Invitational. Specific about what the object means, not how it was produced. Uses second person ("you"). Focuses on the story behind the release, the craft behind the object, the honesty of the transaction. Avoids industry language (no "IP development," no "production pipeline"). Instead: "the world behind it," "the story it comes from," "what you're getting."

Example register: "Objects crafted with the same care as the worlds they come from. Limited drops tied to real collaborations. You know what you're getting. No blind boxes. No tricks."

### For Creators and Studios

Direct. Respectful of their craft. Leads with the creator's problem (losing control, flattening the IP, becoming a production company), then shows how the label model solves it. Uses specific terms. Does not oversell. Frames Vixio as a creative partner, not a vendor or service provider.

Example register: "You built the world. A merch company will print your logo on their products and move on. A label invests in the world itself. Creative approval stays with you. Your name stays on it. Think A24 directors: the director's vision is the product."

### Social Media

Compressed. Visual-first (the copy supports the image or video, not the other way around). No marketing fluff. No hashtag pileups. One idea per post. Fragments are fine. The goal is to stop the scroll with a specific image or claim, not to explain the business.

Example register: "A24 for film. Pop Mart for designer toys. We asked: what does this model look like for creator-led physical IP?"

### Process / Behind-the-Scenes

Maker's voice. Shows the work: materials, prototypes, dead ends, craft decisions. Honest about what is hard. Does not perform struggle. Uses first person when it is Denis speaking directly. Names specific tools, materials, and constraints.

Example register: "First sample came back wrong. The weight felt cheap. Switched from hollow-cast to solid resin at 2x the unit cost. If it doesn't feel right in the hand, we don't ship it."

## 5. Vocabulary

### Words We Use Freely

release, collaboration, artifact, label, crafted, limited drops, honest pricing, narrative-driven, creator partnership, IP, world, characters, taste, standard, production, creative direction, physical form, curated

### Words We Use Sparingly

immersive (acceptable in public copy but not as the lead descriptor), collectible (technically correct but risks sounding like mass merch; pair with specifics), experience (too generic when used alone; pair with specifics)

### Words We Never Use

metaverse, innovative, cutting-edge, synergy, disrupt, transform, leverage, unlock, elevate, seamless, holistic, ecosystem (as marketing buzzword), passion/passionate, excited to share, game-changing, next-level, revolutionary, merch (when describing our own products), content (when describing creative work)

## 6. Rhythm Rules

### Sentence Length Variation

Alternate between long and short. A paragraph of all short sentences sounds like a telegram. A paragraph of all long sentences sounds like a whitepaper. The Vixio rhythm is: medium setup, short punch, long detail, short close.

Good rhythm: "Creators pour years into building worlds. Characters with arcs. Environments with logic. Stories that earn their payoffs. Then the physical activation is a logo on a mug."

That is: one long sentence (the setup), three fragments (the evidence), one long sentence (the consequence).

### Paragraph Structure

Lead with the claim or the problem. Support with specifics. Close with the sharpest version of the point. Do not bury the lead. Do not end paragraphs with qualifiers, caveats, or softening language.

### Specifics Replace Adjectives

Never write "growing market" when you can write "48% already purchasing." Never write "significant demand" when you can write "84% plan to buy." Never write "premium quality" when you can write "solid resin, hand-painted, 200g." The number or the material is always more credible and more memorable than the adjective.

### Punctuation

No em dashes. Use commas, periods, or restructure the sentence. Colons are acceptable for lists and definitions. Semicolons are acceptable sparingly. Exclamation marks are never used in body copy. Question marks are rare and only appear in direct, rhetorical questions that the next sentence answers.

### Whitespace

Short paragraphs. One idea per paragraph. Let the page breathe. Dense walls of text violate the directed attention principle. If a paragraph has more than four sentences, it probably contains two ideas and should be split.

## 7. The Goosebump Standard

The Goosebump Standard is Vixio's creative benchmark. It comes from specific reference works: the Reiner/Bertholt reveal in Attack on Titan, Zimmer's single sustained piano note, Tommy vs Alfie in Peaky Blinders, the final act of Arcane. What these share is not spectacle. It is directed attention, controlled pacing, intentional silence, and earned climax.

Applied to writing, the Goosebump Standard means:

### Directed Attention

Every sentence should point the reader's focus to one thing. Do not scatter attention across multiple claims in a single statement. "A merch company prints your logo on their products. A label invests in your world." works because each sentence holds exactly one idea and the contrast does the work.

### Controlled Pacing

Build tension by withholding the resolution. State the problem. Let it sit. Add evidence. Let it compound. Then, and only then, deliver the position. The problem statement on the website does this: paragraphs of problem before the closing line.

### Intentional Silence

Not every idea needs explanation. Some lines are better when they stand alone without elaboration. "Limited drops. Honest pricing. No tricks." does not need a follow-up paragraph explaining what that means. Trust the reader.

### Earned Climax

The strongest line in any section should come last, and it should feel inevitable rather than surprising. It is earned by the buildup that precedes it. Do not put your best line first and then explain it. Build to it.

Bad: "Nobody offers this. Here is why: merch companies flatten the IP. Agencies leave after the event. Building in-house turns a creator into a production company."
Good: "Merch companies flatten the IP. Agencies leave after the event. Building in-house turns a creator into a production company. Nobody offers a creator full production capability and creative integrity in the same partnership. That gap is where we build."

## 8. Anti-Patterns

Specific writing habits that violate the Vixio voice. If you catch yourself doing any of these, rewrite.

### 8.1 The Adjective Pile

"Our innovative, cutting-edge, premium collectibles deliver transformative results."

Every adjective weakens the sentence. Replace with one specific fact.

### 8.2 The Excitement Declaration

"We're excited to announce..." / "We're thrilled to share..." / "We're passionate about..."

Vixio does not declare its emotions. It shows its work.

### 8.3 The Consultant Hedge

"We believe that..." / "We think that..." / "It's possible that..."

State the position directly. "The through-line is taste, not genre." Not "We believe that a taste-driven approach may offer advantages over genre-specific positioning."

### 8.4 The Feature Dump

Listing capabilities without connecting them to outcomes or showing why they matter.

Bad: "We offer creative direction, design, manufacturing, distribution, marketing, and IP development."
Good: "Concept to finished product: creative direction, design, manufacturing, distribution. You stay creative. We handle the build."

### 8.5 The Merch Voice

Generic product photography, "limited edition" as pure marketing, random-pull mechanics, countdown timers for artificial scarcity, phrases like "Collect them all!" or "Which one are you?" Vixio produces artifacts tied to real collaborations with real creators, not disposable goods with manufactured urgency.

### 8.6 The Generic Agency Closer

"Let's create something amazing together." / "Ready to elevate your brand?" / "Partner with us to unlock new possibilities."

Instead, close with a specific next step or a sharp restatement of the value: "You bring the vision. We handle the build. Your name stays on it."

### 8.7 The Buzzword Substitution

Replacing a real explanation with a branded term. "Our proprietary Creator Synergy Framework delivers..." This is consulting-speak. Vixio explains what it does in plain terms: "Full production partnership. Creative approval stays with you. One collaboration at a time."

### 8.8 Deficit Framing

"Although we're a new label..." / "Despite not having shipped yet..." / "We may be small, but..."

Never frame the current stage as a limitation. State what exists: "First collaboration in production. The label is only as real as what it ships."

### 8.9 The Wall of Text

Paragraphs longer than five sentences. Dense blocks without visual breaks. Multiple ideas crammed into one section. This violates the directed attention principle. Split it. Let it breathe.

### 8.10 Em Dash Addiction

Using em dashes to jam two clauses together. Restructure as two sentences or use a colon. The em dash is banned from all Vixio copy.

## Usage Notes

This profile is designed to be self-contained. A writer or AI agent should be able to produce on-brand Vixio copy using only this document, without access to the original website components or brand book.

When in doubt, apply this test: does the copy sound like a creative director explaining their label's standards to a peer, or does it sound like a salesperson pitching to a prospect? Vixio always sounds like the former.

The voice is Denis Tam's voice, formalized. Direct. Opinionated. Specific. Honest about what exists and what does not. Never defensive. Never performative. The craft speaks.
