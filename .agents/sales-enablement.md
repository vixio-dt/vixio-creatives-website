## Document 2: Partnership FAQ

---

**What does Vixio actually do?**

We are a creative label. We find creators with worlds we believe in and produce physical products tied to those worlds. We handle production end to end: creative development, manufacturing, quality control, distribution. The creator brings the vision. We handle the build. The product ships under the Vixio label with the creator's name on it.

We are not an agency. We don't execute briefs. We are not a merch company. We don't put your logo on commodity products. We partner with IPs we want to invest in long-term.

---

**What IP rights do I keep?**

Your IP stays yours. Vixio does not acquire your characters, your world, or your story. The specific terms of each collaboration (production rights, distribution rights, duration) are negotiated per project. But the underlying IP ownership does not transfer to Vixio.

For comparison: Pop Mart artists retain IP ownership but grant exclusive global distribution rights for specific toy designs for 24 months with non-compete clauses. We aim for terms that are more flexible than that, though the specifics depend on the collaboration.

---

**How does revenue work?**

Revenue is shared between Vixio and the creator. The exact split is negotiated per collaboration based on what each side brings: if you are contributing a fully developed IP with a following and we are handling production and distribution, that is a different arrangement than co-developing something from scratch.

We are transparent about this: we are pre-launch, so we do not have a standard rate card yet. Early partners will have more room to negotiate favorable terms because they are helping build the label's credibility.

For context: Pop Mart pays artists 8-12% royalties on net revenue (standard), with established names getting hybrid deals of $50K-$500K upfront plus 5-8%. We believe creators deserve a more equitable share when the collaboration is a genuine partnership.

---

**What if I already have merch deals?**

That depends on the terms of your existing deals. If you have an exclusive arrangement with another company (like Pop Mart's 24-month exclusive distribution contracts), you would need to check whether a Vixio collaboration conflicts.

If your existing deals are non-exclusive, or cover different product categories, there is likely room. We are not trying to replace your existing revenue streams. We are offering something different: crafted products tied to your world, not commodity merch with your logo on it.

Bring the specifics. We will figure it out together or tell you honestly if the timing does not work.

---

**What does "limited drops" mean?**

It means we produce a defined quantity and that is it. No restocking to chase demand. No inflating supply to maximize revenue. The product is available for a window, and then it is done.

This is not manufactured scarcity for its own sake. It is a production model that lets us maintain quality and keep each release feeling intentional. Your fans know they are getting something crafted, not mass-produced.

---

**How long does a collaboration take?**

Expect 4-8 months from agreement to shipped product, depending on complexity. That covers co-development, prototyping, manufacturing, and distribution prep.

For reference: Pop Mart's pipeline is 10-14 months from submission to launch. Our smaller scale means we can move faster, but we will not rush past quality to hit a date.

---

**What's your manufacturing capability?**

We have a family manufacturing network in Dongguan, which is the global hub for designer toy and collectible production. This gives us access to the same manufacturing base that Pop Mart uses (they acquired stakes in two Shenzhen mold-making facilities), but through relationship-based access rather than corporate infrastructure.

This means: vinyl figures, resin pieces, packaging, assembly. The medium is not locked in advance. If the collaboration calls for something different, we source it.

What we do not have: Pop Mart's scale. They produce millions of units across 500+ stores. We produce limited runs at high quality. That is a deliberate choice.

---

**Why should I partner with a label that hasn't shipped yet?**

Fair question. Three reasons:

1. **You shape the label.** Our first collaborations define what Vixio becomes. That is real influence, not a marketing line. A24's early directors (Barry Jenkins, the Safdies, Ari Aster) defined what "an A24 film" meant. Our early partners define what "a Vixio release" means.

2. **Early-mover terms.** When the label has a portfolio and a reputation, we negotiate from a different position. Right now, we are building credibility together, and the terms reflect that.

3. **The model is proven.** Taste-as-brand works. A24 proved it for film. Pop Mart proved it for designer toys. What is new is applying it to creator-led physical IP with honest commerce and narrative depth. The risk is execution, not concept.

If you need a proven track record before engaging, that is completely reasonable. We will still be here when the first release ships.

---

**What happens if it doesn't sell?**

We share the risk. The specific terms depend on the deal structure, but Vixio absorbs production costs. You are not stuck with unsold inventory or a bill for manufacturing.

If a release underperforms, we learn from it. The limited-drop model means exposure is bounded: we are not sitting on 100,000 units. We are producing a defined run, selling it, and assessing together.

---

**Who else are you working with?**

We are currently producing our first collaboration. We are not announcing names until we have something to show. We are talking to several creators in Hong Kong's art toy, illustration, and indie game communities.

If you need social proof before committing, we understand. We would rather be honest about where we are than name-drop people who have not shipped with us yet.

## Document 3: Objection Handler / Conversation Guide

For Denis to reference during live conversations. Each section: the objection, the real concern, the honest response, and the pivot.

---

### "I can just do merch myself."

**The real concern:** They do not want to give up control or margin to a partner when they could keep 100%.

**Honest response:** You can. And for some things, you should. Print-on-demand works fine for t-shirts, stickers, and posters. But if you want to produce quality physical products -- vinyl figures, resin art pieces, crafted objects -- you need a production pipeline: prototyping, tooling, factory relationships, quality control, packaging, distribution. That is a different company than the one you are running. Most creators who try to build that in-house end up spending more time on production management than on creating. We handle the build so you can stay creative. The question is whether the product you want to make is something you can produce yourself at the quality your world deserves.

**Pivot:** What kind of physical product have you imagined for your IP? Have you looked into what it would take to produce it independently?

---

### "Pop Mart already approached me."

**The real concern:** They have a proven, scaled option on the table and need a reason to consider an unknown alternative.

**Honest response:** Pop Mart is the biggest in the world. If scale and reach are your priority, they are hard to beat: 500+ stores, 30+ countries, established manufacturing. But here is what you are agreeing to. Your character goes into a blind-box format. Your fans will not know what they are getting when they buy it. You will receive 8-12% royalties while Pop Mart captures 66%+ gross margins. You sign a 24-month exclusive with a non-compete. And your IP becomes an aesthetic collectible -- no story, no world, just a figure in a sealed box alongside chase variants and manufactured scarcity.

With us, your world stays intact. Your fans know exactly what they are buying. The partnership is built around your narrative, not around a gambling mechanic. And we believe you deserve a larger share of the value your IP creates.

We are not asking you to choose us over Pop Mart today. We are asking you to consider what kind of physical expression your world deserves.

**Pivot:** What matters more to you right now: maximum distribution or creative integrity in how your world shows up physically?

---

### "I've never heard of you."

**The real concern:** No track record. No social proof. Why take a risk on an unknown?

**Honest response:** That is fair. We are pre-launch. Our first collaboration is in production. There is no portfolio to show you yet. What I can tell you is what we are building: a creative label for entertainment IPs. Think A24's model for physical products. Taste-curated, creator-first, limited drops, honest commerce.

The model is not new. A24 proved taste-as-brand works for film. Pop Mart proved it for designer toys. What is new is applying it to creator-led physical IP without blind boxes, without extractive terms, and with the creator's world kept intact.

Our early partners define the label. That is a genuine advantage: you are not joining a roster of 50 creators. You are helping build what this thing becomes. A24's first directors shaped what "an A24 film" meant. Our first collaborations shape what "a Vixio release" means.

If you need to see us ship before engaging, I respect that. We will reach out again when the first release is in hand.

**Pivot:** I am happy to share more about the production capability, the supply chain, and what the first collaboration looks like. What would help you evaluate whether this is worth a deeper conversation?

---

### "What's in it for me financially?"

**The real concern:** They want specific numbers. How much money, how soon, what is the split.

**Honest response:** I will be direct. Revenue is shared, and the split is negotiated per collaboration based on what each side brings. I cannot give you a standard rate card because we have not shipped yet. What I can tell you is the landscape:

Pop Mart pays 8-12% royalties to new artists. Established names get hybrid deals: $50K-$500K upfront plus 5-8% backend. Kenny Wong got 20% as the founding partnership, but that is the exception.

We believe creators deserve a more equitable share. The specifics depend on the deal: if you bring a developed IP with a following and we handle production and distribution, that is one structure. If we are co-developing something from scratch, that is different.

What I can promise: our terms will be transparent, you will understand the economics before you commit, and we will not hide behind industry-standard language to pay you less than you are worth.

Early partners also get better terms. When the label has a track record, we negotiate differently.

**Pivot:** What does a good outcome look like for you financially? Let us start from what you need and work backwards.

---

### "I don't want to give up creative control."

**The real concern:** They have seen what happens when IPs get handed to production companies. The work gets flattened, cheapened, or misrepresented.

**Honest response:** Neither do we. The entire point of this label is that the creator's vision is the product. You have creative approval on everything that ships. Your name is on it. If something does not meet the standard you set for your world, it does not go out.

This is not a licensing deal where you hand over assets and see the result six months later. It is a co-development process. You are involved in design, prototyping, and final approval. We handle the production execution, but the creative direction comes from you.

Think about how A24 works with directors. The director's vision is the film. A24 does not impose notes or demand test-screening rewrites. That is the relationship we are building: you direct, we produce.

**Pivot:** What specifically are you most protective of in your IP? That helps us understand where the creative guardrails need to be.

---

### "My IP isn't ready for physical products."

**The real concern:** They think they need a fully developed merchandise line or product concept before approaching a production partner.

**Honest response:** You do not need a product concept. You need a world worth building in. If you have characters with depth, environments with logic, a story that people care about, that is enough to start the conversation.

The medium emerges from the collaboration. We do not start with "let us make a vinyl figure." We start with "what is this world, and what physical form does it deserve?" Maybe it is a figure. Maybe it is an art book. Maybe it is something we have not done before.

Some of the best physical expressions of IP come from worlds that were never designed for products. Genuine Studio makes narrative visual novels set in Hong Kong. Those characters and worlds could become crafted physical pieces that fans hold onto. The IP does not need to be "product-ready." It needs to be rich enough to deserve a physical form.

**Pivot:** Tell me about your world. What do fans respond to most? What would they want to hold in their hands?

---

### "I'm already locked into an exclusive deal."

**The real concern:** They are interested but contractually constrained. Or they are using this as a polite way to decline.

**Honest response:** That is fine. Exclusivity terms have end dates. If you are under a 24-month exclusive with Pop Mart or another partner, let us stay in touch and revisit when that window closes.

In the meantime, there may be ways to collaborate that do not conflict with your existing terms. If the exclusive covers specific product categories (for example, vinyl figures), there may be room in other formats. If it is a full-category exclusive, we wait.

I would rather build a relationship now that turns into a collaboration later than force something that creates legal complications for you.

**Pivot:** When does your current deal come up for renewal? And is the exclusive product-specific or broad?

## Usage Notes

**For the one-pager:** Send after a first conversation or warm intro. It is designed to be read in two minutes and forwarded to a collaborator or manager. Can be converted to PDF as-is.

**For the FAQ:** Reference during conversation prep. The answers are written in Denis's voice so he can paraphrase naturally. Do not send the full FAQ document to creators -- it is a reference, not a handout.

**For the objection handler:** Keep accessible during calls and meetings. The "real concern" framing helps Denis respond to what the creator actually means, not just what they said. The pivots move the conversation forward without pressuring.

**General guidance:**
- Lead with curiosity about their world, not with the Vixio pitch.
- The best conversations start with "tell me about your IP" and end with "here is what we could build together."
- Pre-launch honesty is an asset, not a liability. Creators respect it because everyone else oversells.
- Do not badmouth competitors. State what Vixio does differently and let the creator draw conclusions.
- If a creator is not ready or not interested, leave the door open. The HK creative community is small. Reputation compounds.
